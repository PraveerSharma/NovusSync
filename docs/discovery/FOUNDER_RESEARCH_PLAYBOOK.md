# Founder Research and Design-Partner Playbook

**Status:** Ready for the founder's one-week outreach, training, and research sprint  
**Audience:** Founder and the two named pilot operators  
**Target participant:** Owner or final decision-maker of an independent yoga studio in Bengaluru Urban  
**Purpose:** Complete honest customer research, identify five credible design partners, and collect the evidence required for Gate G0  
**Important:** This is a field guide, not a contract, privacy notice, invoice, or legal promise.

## 1. The goal in simple language

Before building the application, prove four things:

1. Yoga-studio owners repeatedly struggle with slow or fragmented Lead response and follow-up.
2. They also struggle to connect a campaign to a booked and genuinely attended trial class.
3. At least five eligible studios will commit time, access, baseline evidence, and two intended campaign cycles.
4. At least two will make an approved paid or deposit-backed commitment.

The founder's job is not to convince every owner. The job is to discover the truth quickly enough to decide whether the product deserves to be built.

## 2. Gate G0 numbers

| Evidence | Required result |
|---|---|
| Eligible-owner interviews | At least 10 unique owner-operated studio records; continue toward 20 while new material findings are still appearing |
| Repeated pain | At least 70% give behavioral evidence of both approved pains |
| Design partners | Five signed two-cycle participation commitments |
| Baseline-ready partners | At least three have usable four-week baseline plus channel, booking, and outcome readiness |
| Paid commitment | At least two pay the first cycle or the approved INR 1,000 credited deposit |
| Manual workflow | Two eligible studios complete a documented campaign-to-attendance observation |
| Prototype tests | Five eligible owners complete the five prototype tasks |
| Decision | Founder records `GO`, `NO-GO`, or `REWORK` with contradictions and rationale |

Do not lower these numbers because an owner is friendly, enthusiastic, or impressed by the prototype.

One `STU-###` code represents one owner-operated business across all of its locations. Only its first valid included eligible interview enters the denominator. Keep a genuine repeat/follow-up row only with an explicit exclusion reason; never use an exclusion to remove an eligible `NO` response.

## 3. Non-negotiable research rules

- Research first. Pitch only after the owner has described the current workflow.
- Ask about the most recent real event, not what the owner thinks they would do.
- Do not lead the participant toward the two target pains.
- Do not argue with counter-evidence. Strong existing workflows are useful evidence.
- Do not promise Leads, bookings, attendance, enrollment, revenue, platform approval, automation, or a launch date.
- Do not ask for customer names, phone numbers, raw chats, passwords, health details, or account credentials.
- Use written notes by default. Record audio/video only after explicit permission and approved private storage/retention.
- Stop outreach immediately after a decline or opt-out.
- A compliment is not demand. A signed commitment, usable baseline, or approved payment is evidence.
- Keep identities and private evidence in the restricted Google Drive workspace. Git receives studio codes and aggregate results only.

## 5. One-week field plan

### Preparation day

- Create the restricted Drive folder and Sheet.
- Import the blank templates.
- Assign studio codes `STU-001`, `STU-002`, and so on.
- Review the product boundaries, price, manual-channel rules, and prohibited promises.
- Complete the [synthetic founder/operator rehearsal](SYNTHETIC_REHEARSAL.md), including one supportive owner, one skeptical owner, and one ineligible contact.
- Open the [synthetic prototype](../../prototype/README.md) and complete all five tasks once.

### Day 1: Warm network first

- Ask known yoga instructors for owner introductions.
- Add 25–30 possible studios to the private prospect list.
- Send the founder's first warm messages.
- Schedule the earliest interviews; do not send the design-partner pitch yet.

### Days 2–3: Research conversations

- Founder leads the first five interviews.
- Operators may schedule, take coded notes, and manage follow-ups without impersonating the founder.
- Score each call immediately while details are fresh.
- Ask each eligible participant for one relevant owner referral.

### Days 4–5: Complete the first evidence batch

- Continue interviews toward at least 10 included unique eligible owner-operated studios.
- Start prototype tests with owners who have finished the research interview.
- Share the design-partner brief only with eligible owners who show a real problem and decision authority.
- Identify candidates for two studio-controlled manual workflow observations.

### Days 6–7: Follow-up and evidence review

- Send the final respectful follow-ups.
- Review both-pain counts, new findings, baseline access, objections, and contradictions.
- Classify serious design-partner candidates.
- Do not request signatures or money until the approved counsel/accountant documents and route exist.
- Prepare a coded weekly summary for the Gate G0 register.

If fewer than 10 included unique eligible studio-owner records fit into one week, continue field work. The calendar does not lower the evidence threshold.

## 6. Founder and operator training

| Time | Exercise |
|---|---|
| 10 minutes | Explain the target owner, target pains, attended-trial outcome, and MVP exclusions |
| 10 minutes | Explain research-first behavior and how leading questions create false evidence |
| 15 minutes | Role-play the interview using the most recent campaign and enquiry |
| 10 minutes | Practice the 60-second pitch and three difficult objections |
| 10 minutes | Complete the five prototype tasks without coaching |
| 5 minutes | Review privacy, opt-out, evidence storage, and escalation rules |

### Founder responsibilities

- Send first messages to warm contacts.
- Lead the first five interviews.
- Give the pitch after research.
- Own pricing and design-partner discussions.
- Decide whether a contradiction changes the product hypothesis.
- Approve any external contact, signature request, payment request, or live-data step.

### Operator responsibilities

- Use individual identities and disclose their role.
- Schedule calls and maintain coded tracker status.
- Take factual notes with permission.
- Never pitch, promise, discount, collect payment, or request access unless explicitly authorized.
- Escalate privacy, claims, complaints, account access, and commercial questions to the founder.

## 7. Eligibility check before scheduling

Confirm what you reasonably can from the introduction or public business information:

- [ ] Independent owner-operated yoga studio.
- [ ] Located in Bengaluru Urban.
- [ ] Owner or final decision-maker can attend.
- [ ] Offers an introductory class, pass, consultation, or similar attended outcome.
- [ ] Uses or can reasonably operate a professional Instagram account.
- [ ] Uses or can reasonably operate an official WhatsApp Business number.
- [ ] Has an external booking route or consistent manual booking process.
- [ ] Business is not primarily medical, therapeutic, child-directed, a franchise, marketplace, gym, or broad wellness service.

Unclear items can be confirmed at the start of the interview. Do not silently broaden eligibility.

> Hi [owner name], this is my final follow-up. I am completing the first set of Bengaluru yoga-studio interviews and would still value your experience. If the timing is not right, no action is needed and I will not follow up again.

> Thank you. Is there one other independent Bengaluru yoga-studio owner who sees enough Instagram or WhatsApp enquiries to have a useful view on this workflow? Please introduce us only if you are comfortable and they are open to it.

## 9. Interview opening

Use this wording before taking notes:

> Thank you for speaking with me. I am researching the current process before building the product. I want to understand what actually happened in your most recent campaign, including anything that works well. I do not need customer names or private messages. May I take written notes and use an anonymous summary for product decisions?

If the owner says no, do not retain interview notes beyond what is required to close the conversation.

Neutral responses during research:

- “Please tell me what happened next.”
- “Can you give me the most recent example?”
- “How did you know that?”
- “Where was that information recorded?”
- “Who did that step?”
- “How long did that take?”
- “What happened when it went wrong?”
- “What already works well?”
- “I am not trying to prove a particular answer. The counterexample is useful.”

Avoid praise such as “Exactly, that is the problem we solve” during the research section.

## 10. Complete owner interview questions

### A. Owner and studio context

1. Please describe the studio, its locations, and the instructors involved.
2. What introductory experience do new prospects usually book first?
3. Who makes the final decisions on marketing, pricing, account access, and customer follow-up?
4. Which Instagram account does the business use?
5. Does the business use the official WhatsApp Business app? Who monitors it?
6. How are trial classes booked today?
7. Which system or person owns schedules, locations, instructors, class capacity, payments, rescheduling, and cancellations?

### B. Most recent campaign

1. Think about your most recent Instagram campaign or promotion that produced an enquiry. What was the offer and when did it run?
2. Who planned and created the campaign?
3. Who approved the facts, price, content, and final post?
4. How and when was it published?
5. Where did the first enquiry arrive: form, WhatsApp, Instagram, phone, or somewhere else?
6. Please walk me through everything that happened from that enquiry onward.
7. What information had to be copied, remembered, or requested again?
8. How did the team know which campaign or offer caused the enquiry?
9. What part of this process worked well?
10. What part took the most effort or caused uncertainty?

### C. Lead response and follow-up

1. Who noticed the most recent enquiry?
2. Roughly how long did the first meaningful response take?
3. Was that during normal service hours?
4. How did the person responding know the campaign offer and context?
5. What questions did the team ask before offering a trial booking?
6. Where were answers and follow-up status recorded?
7. How did another person take over if the first person was unavailable?
8. Can you recall a recent enquiry that was late, missed, duplicated, or lost? What happened?
9. How often does something like that occur?
10. What did it cost in owner/staff time, customer experience, or lost opportunity?
11. What happens after a prospect says “not now,” asks to stop, or stops replying?
12. What have you already tried to improve follow-up?

### D. Booking and attended outcome

1. How does a qualified person see available introductory classes?
2. How do they choose a location, instructor, or time?
3. Who or what prevents double booking and over-capacity classes?
4. How are reschedules, cancellations, payments, and waitlists handled?
5. How do you know whether a booked person actually attended?
6. Who is allowed to confirm attendance or no-show?
7. Can you connect that attendance back to the exact campaign and offer?
8. Please describe the last time you tried to do that.
9. What happens when source, booking, or attendance evidence conflicts?
10. Which campaign numbers do you trust today?
11. Which numbers do you wish you trusted more?

### E. Four-week baseline and workload

1. For the most recent four weeks, which of these can you retrieve: enquiries, first-response time, qualified Leads, bookings, attendance, no-shows, enrollments/losses, ad spend, and owner/staff time?
2. Where is each number stored?
3. Who can verify each number?
4. Which numbers are estimates rather than recorded facts?
5. How much owner/staff time per week goes into content, replies, follow-up, booking reconciliation, and reporting?
6. What would make it difficult to provide a minimized baseline and anonymous outcome summary?
7. How long would it take to prepare that baseline?

### F. Priority, current alternatives, and buying process

1. Compared with other problems in the business, how important is this one?
2. What problems are currently more urgent?
3. What software, agency, spreadsheet, or manual process do you use now?
4. What do you pay for those tools or services?
5. What works well enough that you would not replace it?
6. What caused you to stop using any previous solution?
7. If nothing changes for three months, what happens?
8. Who else must approve a pilot, account access, data use, or payment?
9. What would make a pilot too risky or burdensome?

Stop the research questions here. Summarize what you heard before pitching:

> Let me check that I understood. Your current process is [short neutral summary]. The main difficulty appears to be [their words], while [what already works] should stay unchanged. Is that accurate? What did I miss?

## 11. Pitching prompts

Pitch only after the research section and only when the owner is eligible.

### 20-second explanation

> We are testing a managed campaign-to-attendance workflow for independent yoga studios. It helps carry one approved offer from Instagram content through enquiry, human follow-up, the studio's booking route, and verified trial attendance, while the owner keeps control.

### 60-second founder pitch

> We are selecting five Bengaluru yoga studios for a hands-on design-partner pilot. Each cycle lasts up to 28 days and focuses on one introductory offer and one outcome: a qualified person attending a trial class. We prepare six Instagram content items, a hosted campaign form, a manual WhatsApp playbook, and an evidence-based weekly review. The studio approves all facts and exact content. Humans publish Instagram posts and send WhatsApp messages through the official apps. Your existing booking system remains in charge of schedules, capacity, and payments. We do not guarantee Leads, attendance, enrollment, or revenue. The first-five price is INR 4,999 through 100 new Leads or INR 6,999 for 101–200, with an INR 1,000 deposit credited to cycle one after the documents and payment route are approved.

### Three-minute design-partner pitch

> The problem we are testing is not simply content creation. Most studios already know how to post. The gap is what happens after the post: enquiries arrive in different places, context gets lost, follow-up depends on whoever notices, booking lives somewhere else, and attendance is rarely tied back to the exact campaign.
>
> In one 28-day cycle, we work with you on one introductory offer. We prepare four single-image posts, two carousels, a hosted form, and a WhatsApp response playbook. You approve every business fact, price, claim, and exact content version. An authorized human publishes and messages manually. We do not ask for passwords and there is no Meta automation.
>
> Your booking provider or existing booking process remains authoritative. We do not recreate schedules, instructor availability, class capacity, payments, or cancellations. We record the evidence from campaign source through booking and owner-verified attendance, then review what worked, what is missing, and one possible next experiment.
>
> We need design partners who will intend to complete two cycles, share a usable four-week baseline, allow governed account and outcome readiness checks, review work on time, and tell us where the process fails. The first-five pricing is INR 4,999 plus applicable tax through 100 new Leads, or INR 6,999 for 101–200. The INR 1,000 commitment deposit is credited to cycle one. Advertising and booking-provider charges stay separate.
>
> This is a measured, product-assisted managed pilot. It is not a promise of results, a replacement for your booking system, or an automated marketing agency. If this fits, the next step is to review the one-page brief and complete a readiness check. We will only request a signature or payment using the final approved documents and route.

### Questions immediately after the pitch

1. What part sounds most useful?
2. What feels unclear, risky, or unnecessary?
3. Which part would create the most extra work for your team?
4. Which part conflicts with how your studio already operates?
5. At the stated price and operating model, what would have to be true for you to complete two cycles?
6. Who else needs to review the brief, account requirements, privacy terms, or payment?
7. Would you like to review the design-partner brief and prototype? Why or why not?
8. What exact next step are you willing to take, and by when?

### Written follow-up after the pitch

> Thank you for the honest discussion. Based on the workflow you described, I am sending the design-partner brief and synthetic prototype for review. This is not yet a contract or payment request. If the model still fits after your review, the next step is a readiness conversation covering baseline data, Instagram/WhatsApp ownership, booking route, attendance evidence, capacity, and the final participation/privacy documents.

## 12. Honest objection handling

Do not “overcome” objections. Clarify them and record the evidence.

| Owner says | Founder response | Follow-up question |
|---|---|---|
| “We already use an agency.” | “That may be a strong alternative. We are testing whether the campaign-to-attendance evidence and owner-control workflow adds anything your agency does not.” | “Please show me how the agency connects the latest campaign to verified attendance and what remains manual for you.” |
| “We already use a CRM or booking tool.” | “We do not want to replace your booking system or full CRM. Your existing route stays authoritative.” | “Where does campaign source or WhatsApp context get lost between those tools, if anywhere?” |
| “I do not share passwords.” | “Good. We prohibit shared passwords. Any later operation uses individual delegated access through official accounts.” | “Does your current Instagram setup support appropriate delegated access for a named operator?” |
| “I do not want you reading private WhatsApp chats.” | “The initial model is human-operated in the official app. We minimize what is recorded and do not treat raw chats as product or AI data.” | “What evidence would you be comfortable recording to verify response, purpose, opt-out, and outcome?” |
| “Can AI publish and reply automatically?” | “No, not in this pilot. AI may propose private drafts, but authorized humans approve and act.” | “Is manual operation acceptable for two measured cycles if it proves the workflow first?” |
| “Can you guarantee Leads or sales?” | “No. We do not guarantee Leads, bookings, attendance, enrollment, or revenue.” | “Which evidence would make the pilot valuable even without a guaranteed result?” |
| “The price is too high.” | “Thank you. The first-five terms are fixed for this test, and price rejection is important evidence.” | “What do you compare it with, and what price or outcome would make the tradeoff reasonable?” |
| “Can we test it for free?” | “You can participate in research and prototype testing without payment. A design-partner place requires the approved paid or credited-deposit commitment because we need real willingness-to-pay evidence.” | “Would you still review the workflow and explain what prevents a paid commitment?” |
| “Just send me the details.” | “I can send the one-page brief, but the owner interview matters because we do not want to assume your workflow.” | “Can we schedule 25 minutes to walk through the most recent campaign before discussing a pilot?” |
| “We do not have four weeks of data.” | “That may mean the studio is not baseline-ready yet. We will not invent it.” | “Which fields can you retrieve, from where, and how long would the missing baseline take to establish?” |
| “We do not use WhatsApp Business.” | “The initial pilot requires an official Business number and a safe human-operated route before activation.” | “Would you be willing and able to establish that with clear ownership and recovery?” |
| “Our bookings happen manually.” | “A manual route can be acceptable if it is stable, authorized, measurable, and rehearsed. We do not force an API.” | “Who owns capacity and how can booking and attendance be evidenced consistently?” |
| “We also need Facebook, email, or other channels.” | “Those channels are outside the initial pilot. We are testing one focused Instagram-to-form/WhatsApp route first.” | “Would the narrow route still solve enough of the problem to justify two cycles?” |
| “We need therapeutic or medical claims.” | “That is outside this pilot's allowed claim boundary.” | “Can the introductory offer be marketed using factual non-medical class information only?” |
| “We want support all night.” | “The planned human service window is 10:00–20:00 Asia/Kolkata, not 24/7.” | “Would that window cover enough of your actual enquiry volume?” |
| “When can we launch?” | “We will not promise a date before discovery, legal/privacy/account readiness, and production gates pass.” | “Is a staged readiness process acceptable if every blocker and owner is visible?” |

Never invent an answer to a legal, tax, privacy, employment, platform-policy, or security question. Record it and escalate.

## 13. Design-partner qualification

After the interview, classify the studio without the owner present.

### Strong candidate

- Eligible owner and business.
- Behavioral evidence of both pains.
- Owner has authority and a concrete next step.
- Four-week baseline appears usable or can be produced quickly.
- Instagram, official WhatsApp Business, booking, and attendance routes are workable.
- Owner accepts the human-operated boundary and two-cycle intent.
- Stated price is credible enough to continue.

### Needs evidence

- Real problem exists, but baseline, owner authority, account access, booking route, attendance evidence, capacity, price acceptance, or timing is unresolved.
- Keep as a candidate only with a named action and date.

### Not a fit

- Ineligible vertical/geography/business model.
- No attended introductory outcome.
- No evidence of both pains.
- Strong existing process removes the problem.
- Requires prohibited automation, channels, guarantees, claims, or regulated data.
- Cannot support safe account, consent, booking, outcome, or payment readiness.

Do not pressure a weak fit into the cohort to reach five.

## 15. Prototype testing script

Use the [clickable prototype](../../prototype/README.md) after the research interview. Do not demo it first.

### Opening

> This is a fictional, synthetic prototype. It cannot publish, message, book, charge, save data, or contact anyone. We are testing the interface, not you. Please think aloud and try each task without waiting for me to explain. If something is confusing, that is evidence we need.

### Five tasks

1. “The introductory-offer price is wrong. Find the source evidence and correct it.”
2. “A carousel changed after an earlier approval. Review and approve the exact current version.”
3. “Show me who will publish Instagram content and send WhatsApp messages, and explain what the software does.”
4. “Synthetic Lead L-103 asked to stop follow-up. Record the opt-out and explain what stops.”
5. “Verify one attended trial, then accept, reject, or defer the proposed next experiment.”

### Neutral facilitator prompts

- “What are you looking for?”
- “What do you expect that button to do?”
- “What would you do next?”
- “What makes you trust or distrust this status?”
- “What information is missing?”

Do not point, explain a label, or reveal the next screen until the confusion is recorded.

### Questions after the five tasks

1. In your own words, what does this product do?
2. What did you think was automated that is actually manual?
3. Which approval or evidence state felt unclear?
4. Did anything make you worry about account access, privacy, customer control, or responsibility?
5. Which screen would you use most often?
6. Which screen feels unnecessary?
7. What important step from your current workflow is missing?
8. Would you trust a named operator to assist under these controls? Why or why not?
9. Does this feel valuable enough for the stated design-partner price? What is the comparison?

The prototype passes only after five eligible owners test it and every material issue has a disposition.

## 16. Manual workflow observation prompts

Complete this with two eligible studios using studio-controlled or synthetic/de-identified evidence unless the applicable OD-033 live-data requirements pass.

For one recent campaign, observe and time:

1. Select the offer and attended outcome.
2. Verify price, locations, instructors, claims, booking route, and capacity ownership.
3. Prepare and approve exact content.
4. Publish through the official Instagram account by a named human.
5. Capture an enquiry through the hosted-form equivalent or observe the studio's current route.
6. Identify campaign source and consent evidence.
7. Give the first substantive human response.
8. Qualify or escalate sensitive answers.
9. Offer the authoritative booking route.
10. Record booking, reschedule, cancellation, or discrepancy.
11. Verify attended, no-show, or cancelled through an authorized person/source.
12. Produce the funnel and one evidence-based next experiment.

At every step ask:

- Who performed it?
- Which account or system was authoritative?
- How many minutes did it take?
- What information was missing or copied?
- What could go wrong?
- What evidence proves completion?
- What privacy, platform, access, claims, or commercial issue appeared?
- Would the owner accept this workload for two cycles?

## 17. After-call scoring

Complete the private `Interviews` row immediately.

### Pain 1 is `YES` only when

The owner gives a recent real incident or inspectable workflow showing delayed response, missed context, repeated entry, inconsistent handoff, or lost follow-up.

### Pain 2 is `YES` only when

The owner gives a recent real inability or manual burden connecting campaign/offer to booking and verified attendance.

### Baseline is usable only when

Named sources and an authorized person can provide the agreed fields for a defined four-week period. “We probably have it” is a gap.

### Willingness stages

| Stage | Meaning | Counts for Gate G0? |
|---|---|---|
| `0 — Polite interest` | Compliment, curiosity, or request for information | No |
| `1 — Concrete follow-up` | Agrees to prototype/readiness step with owner and date | Shows interest, not commitment |
| `2 — Signed commitment` | Approved participation commitment for two intended cycles | Yes for five-partner threshold |
| `3 — Paid/deposit-backed` | Approved first-cycle payment or INR 1,000 credited deposit completed | Yes for two-partner threshold |

Also record the strongest counter-evidence, current alternative, price response, trust concern, access burden, new material finding, contradiction, and next step.

9. Which legal/privacy/platform/access/commercial blockers appeared?
10. Which decision should remain unchanged, be amended, or be reopened?

Do not record Gate G0 `GO` after one week unless every required evidence row passes.

## 20. Founder quick-reference card

### Before the call

- Owner/final decision-maker confirmed.
- Studio appears eligible.
- Studio code created privately.
- No customer data requested.

### During research

- Ask about the last real campaign.
- Follow the enquiry through response, booking, and attendance.
- Ask who, where, when, how long, and how they know.
- Ask what already works.
- Record counter-evidence.
- Do not pitch early.

### During the pitch

- One offer, one attended outcome, two intended 28-day cycles.
- Owner approves facts and exact content.
- Humans publish and message.
- Existing booking system stays authoritative.
- INR 4,999/6,999 tiers; INR 1,000 credited deposit after approval.
- No guarantees, shared passwords, Meta automation, or 24/7 promise.

### After the call

- Score both pains from behavior, not opinion.
- Record baseline and access gaps.
- Record price reaction and concrete next step.
- Send only approved material.
- Honor decline/opt-out.

## 21. Escalate instead of answering

Stop and ask the founder, counsel, accountant, or technical/security reviewer when the owner asks about:

- legal compliance, DPDP roles, consent wording, DPA, or cross-border processing;
- GST, tax, invoice form, deposit treatment, refund enforceability, or payment collection;
- operator employment/volunteer status, confidentiality, work product, or liability;
- health, therapeutic, child-directed, testimonial, likeness, or guaranteed-result claims;
- shared credentials, account recovery, unauthorized access, security incidents, or data deletion;
- Meta API automation, App Review, WhatsApp API/BSP, provider tokens, or webhooks;
- a new country, currency, payment provider, vertical, channel, CRM, or automation requirement.

Record the question, owner, and required decision. Never invent confidence.

## 22. What to send back after the field week

When the founder completes the outreach week, provide:

- eligible interview count;
- both-pain numerator and denominator;
- material findings and counterexamples;
- partner funnel counts;
- prototype test count and issue summary;
- manual-workflow status;
- unresolved contradictions/blockers; and
- coded evidence references.

Do not send prospect names, contact details, raw messages, passwords, signatures, payment evidence, or customer/Lead data in chat or Git. Those stay in the restricted Drive workspace.
