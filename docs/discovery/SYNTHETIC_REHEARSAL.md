# Synthetic Founder and Operator Rehearsal

**Status:** Ready for training  
**Evidence status:** **Training only—never count this as an interview, workflow, prototype test, partner commitment, or Gate G0 evidence**  
**Participants:** Founder, operator 1, and operator 2  
**Recommended duration:** 75–90 minutes

## 1. Purpose

Use this rehearsal once before the first owner interview. It trains the team to:

- ask neutral questions about recent behavior before pitching;
- recognize eligible and ineligible participants;
- capture both supporting and disconfirming evidence;
- explain the product without promising results or hidden automation;
- handle privacy, access, medical, price, and trust objections safely;
- choose the correct next step without treating interest as commitment; and
- keep private information out of Git and ordinary chat.

Use fictional names and facts. Do not place the rehearsal rows in the live research tabs; otherwise they can accidentally inflate the denominator.

## 2. Setup

1. Open the [founder playbook](FOUNDER_RESEARCH_PLAYBOOK.md), [outcome rule](OUTCOME_DEFINITION_AND_EXAMPLES.md), and [prototype](../../prototype/README.md).
2. Assign one interviewer, one fictional owner, and one observer.
3. The fictional owner reads only their role card. The interviewer must discover the facts through questions.
4. Run a 20-minute conversation: 12 minutes research, 3 minutes explanation/pitch, and 5 minutes next-step discussion.
5. The observer scores the conversation immediately.
6. Rotate roles and use a different card.

## 3. Role cards

### Card A — Real pain, weak measurement

**Reveal only to the fictional owner:**

- You own one Bengaluru yoga studio and make the final buying decision.
- Your last Instagram offer produced 18 enquiries across a form, DMs, and WhatsApp.
- You replied personally when free. Four replies were later than one day; two prospects followed up twice.
- You believe five trials were booked, but you cannot reliably connect them to the campaign.
- Your attendance book shows three attended trials, but no campaign source.
- You use a simple hosted booking link and are willing to show aggregate four-week counts, not customer details.
- You dislike software that sends messages without review.
- You are interested only if manual work stays visible and the owner retains approval.

**Good classification:** Eligible. Behavioral evidence supports both pains. Baseline is partial, not yet ready. Strong interview and prototype-test candidate; design-partner readiness still needs verification.

### Card B — Strong counter-evidence

**Reveal only to the fictional owner:**

- You own a two-location Bengaluru studio and have final authority.
- A trained receptionist responds within ten minutes during opening hours.
- Every campaign uses a unique booking link; the booking provider records source and attendance.
- You can show four-week aggregate counts and rarely lose campaign context.
- Your main problem is class retention after month one, not trial acquisition or follow-up.
- You would not replace the current workflow and would not pay for this pilot.

**Good classification:** Eligible interview, but both target pains are `NO`. This is valuable counter-evidence, not a failed interview. Do not force a design-partner pitch.

### Card C — Friendly but ineligible contact

**Reveal only to the fictional owner:**

- You are a yoga instructor, not the owner or final decision-maker.
- The studio is part of a national franchise; marketing and customer systems are controlled by headquarters.
- You like the concept and repeatedly say the owner will probably like it.
- You can introduce the founder to the local manager but cannot authorize a pilot, evidence access, or payment.

**Good classification:** Ineligible for the owner-interview denominator. Record the referral route, thank the person, and request an introduction. Do not count enthusiasm as demand.

### Card D — Trust, privacy, and guarantee pressure

**Reveal only to the fictional owner:**

- You own an eligible independent studio.
- You ask whether the product needs Instagram and WhatsApp passwords.
- You ask whether AI will answer medical questions and guarantee 20 new members.
- You offer to export raw customer chats and health notes to “make training easier.”
- Your booking is manual but can be evidenced by a studio-controlled register.
- You have real follow-up pain but will proceed only if access, evidence, and responsibility are clear.

**Good classification:** Potentially eligible, with real pain and material readiness questions. The interviewer refuses passwords/raw health data, explains human-only messaging and no guarantee, records the privacy/claims concern, and routes legal or medical questions to qualified humans.

### Card E — Price enthusiasm without commitment

**Reveal only to the fictional owner:**

- You own an eligible studio and describe both pains with recent examples.
- You say INR 4,999 “sounds reasonable” but avoid giving a budget, authority date, or next step.
- You ask for a free six-month trial and a refund if no one enrolls.
- You are willing to test the prototype but not yet to share baseline aggregates or sign anything.

**Good classification:** Evidence supports both pains; willingness stage is verbal interest only. Invite a prototype test and readiness follow-up. Do not record paid intent, commitment, or partner readiness.

## 4. Required interviewer behaviors

The interviewer should:

- verify decision authority and MVP eligibility first;
- ask about the most recent campaign and enquiry;
- ask who did what, in which tool, how long it took, and what evidence exists;
- use neutral probes such as “What happened next?” and “Can you walk me through the last time?”;
- ask for the strongest current workaround and counterexample;
- request only aggregate or studio-controlled evidence at this stage;
- ask permission before taking notes or recording;
- deliver the pitch only after the research section;
- explain manual Instagram/WhatsApp operation, owner approval, external booking ownership, and the absence of outcome guarantees; and
- close with one concrete next step or a respectful stop.

## 5. Automatic rehearsal failures

Repeat the role-play if the interviewer or operator:

- reveals the target pains and asks the owner to agree;
- pitches before learning the current workflow;
- counts a compliment, referral, or price comment as commitment;
- promises Leads, attendance, enrollment, revenue, automation, 24/7 service, or a launch date;
- requests or accepts a password, recovery code, raw customer chat, health detail, or unnecessary customer export;
- records a WhatsApp click as a Lead or a booking as attendance;
- argues with counter-evidence or changes the qualification rule to keep a prospect;
- continues outreach after a decline or opt-out; or
- puts fictional rehearsal data into the real evidence denominator.

## 7. Five-minute prototype rehearsal

After the interview role-play, the fictional owner completes the five prototype tasks without coaching. The facilitator may say only:

- “What do you expect this to do?”
- “What would you try next?”
- “What feels unclear or unsafe?”
- “Please continue as you normally would.”

Record confusion and assistance in a temporary training note. Do not count this as one of the five eligible-owner prototype tests.
